What Is Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic condition where your body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough insulin to maintain normal blood glucose levels. Unlike type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes is largely preventable through lifestyle modifications.

Key Risk Factors for Type 2 Diabetes

  • Age: Risk increases significantly after age 45
  • Weight: Being overweight or obese is a major risk factor
  • Family History: Having a parent or sibling with diabetes increases your risk
  • Physical Inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle contributes to insulin resistance
  • High Blood Pressure: Often associated with diabetes risk
  • Poor Diet: High sugar and processed food consumption
  • Waist Circumference: Excess abdominal fat is particularly concerning

❓ FAQ – Common Questions About Diabetes Risk

Q: Who is at risk for type 2 diabetes?

A: People at higher risk include those over 45, with a family history of diabetes, who are overweight or obese, have high blood pressure, lead a sedentary lifestyle, or have prediabetes. Certain ethnic groups and those with a history of gestational diabetes are also at increased risk.

Q: What are diabetes risk factors?

A: Key risk factors include age (45+), family history, being overweight (BMI 25+), physical inactivity, high blood pressure, poor diet, large waist circumference, and previous elevated blood glucose levels. Some factors like age and family history cannot be changed, but lifestyle factors can be modified.

Q: How to lower diabetes risk?

A: You can lower your risk by maintaining a healthy weight, exercising regularly (150 minutes per week), eating a balanced diet rich in fruits and vegetables, limiting sugar and processed foods, managing stress, getting adequate sleep, and avoiding smoking. Even losing 5-7% of your body weight can significantly reduce risk.

Q: Who is at risk for diabetes?

A: Anyone can develop diabetes, but risk increases with age, family history, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and certain medical conditions. People with prediabetes, gestational diabetes history, or metabolic syndrome are at particularly high risk. Regular screening is important for early detection and prevention.
