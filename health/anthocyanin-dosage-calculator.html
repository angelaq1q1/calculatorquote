❓Common Questions About Using the Anthocyanin Dosage Calculator

Q1: How much anthocyanin should you eat a day?

A: Estimating daily anthocyanin intake is challenging due to incomplete data on their quantities in food. However, it is approximated to be around 12.5 mg/day in the US, ranging from 19 to 65 mg/day for men and 18–44 mg/day for women in Europe.

Q2: Is too much anthocyanin bad for you?

A: Anthocyanin-rich foods are generally considered safe. However, the same may not always be true for anthocyanin supplements, which can provide much larger amounts of polyphenols than you would usually get from a balanced diet.

Q3: What food is highest in anthocyanins?

A: Fruits, vegetables, and grains with red, purple, blue, or black colors tend to be rich in anthocyanins. Berries usually have the highest levels, especially black elderberries and aronia berries (chokeberries). Blueberries, blackberries, raspberries, and strawberries are also excellent sources.

Q4: What do anthocyanins do to the body?

A: Cell studies, animal research, and human clinical trials suggest that anthocyanins have antioxidant and antimicrobial activity, may improve visual and neurological health, and can help protect against several non‑communicable diseases.

Q5: Is anthocyanin good for the kidneys?

A: Some research indicates that certain anthocyanins, such as those from maqui berries, could support kidney function by reducing inflammation and oxidative stress, which are key drivers of chronic kidney disease progression. Their effects on cell adhesion may also help reduce renal fibrosis risk.

Q6: What do anthocyanins do to the brain?

A: Both animal studies and randomized clinical trials show that anthocyanins can enhance cognition and provide neuroprotection. These benefits are linked to their ability to lower oxidative stress, reduce inflammation, and limit apoptosis (cell death) in brain tissue.
