1. Your Fitness Level

If you are less trained, you burn more calories. If you are more trained, your body moves more efficiently and burns slightly less.

2. The Surface Grade

The ground under your feet changes everything. Running uphill makes your body work harder, so you burn more calories. Running downhill lowers the energy cost, but only to a point. After a -10% decline, your legs still need extra control, so the calorie savings stop increasing. This tool adjusts your results so you don't overestimate or underestimate the effect of the terrain.

3. Treadmill vs. Outdoor Running

Running on a treadmill usually burns fewer calories. On the ground, you have to push against air resistance. On a treadmill, air resistance is almost zero. For example, at 9 km/h (5.6 mph), outdoor running burns about 1.2% more energy than treadmill running‚Äîsmall but real. 4. What "Net Calories" Means

The calculator shows net calories burned, which excludes the calories your body needs for basic functions like breathing and digestion. This gives you a clearer picture of what you truly burned because of running, not what you burn at rest.

How Many Calories Does Running Burn?

Running is one of the most effective calorie-burning exercises. Studies show it burns more calories than cycling, dancing, or swimming. The number depends mostly on:

  • Your weight
  • Your running effort
  • The surface grade

Many runners use the "100 calories per mile" rule. It's a simple average, and it works for many people with average body weight. If you weigh more or run less efficiently, you burn more calories. If you weigh less, you burn fewer.

One surprising fact is that running faster does not always mean burning more calories per mile. Your body becomes more efficient at higher speeds, so the calorie cost per distance stays more stable than people expect. That's why this calculator focuses on distance‚Äînot time.

Running for Weight Loss

If your goal is weight loss, running can help, but knowing your calorie burn matters. One pound of fat is about 3,500 calories. To lose one pound a week, you generally need a 500‚Äì1,000 calorie deficit per day. Running contributes to that goal, but food choices matter just as much. A single extra snack can cancel out the calories you burned on a morning run.

The good news is that speed doesn't make or break your results. You can burn the same amount of calories by running slower for longer. The calculator helps you understand your numbers so you can plan smart instead of relying on guesswork.
