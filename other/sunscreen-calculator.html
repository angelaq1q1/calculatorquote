How the Calculator Works

Our calculator uses the 2mg per square centimeter standard. This means you need 2 milligrams of sunscreen for every square centimeter of skin. For your face, we calculate the area using your face width and height. We multiply this area by 0.002 to get the milliliters needed. For your body, we use a formula to find your body surface area. We convert this to square centimeters. Then we multiply by 0.002 to get the base amount. If you wear short sleeves, we reduce the amount by 30 percent. If you wear long sleeves, we reduce it by 50 percent. The calculator also accounts for reapplication. You should reapply every 2 hours. So if you are in the sun for 6 hours, you need 3 applications per day. We multiply your daily amount by applications per day. Then we multiply by the number of days. This gives you the total amount needed for your trip.

Q1. How much sunscreen do I need for my whole body?

A: You need about 2.9 ml for each arm, 5.8 ml for each leg, 12 ml for your torso front and back, and 0.35 ml for your face. This adds up to about 29.75 ml or 1.2 fl oz for your whole body each time you apply. Our calculator gives you a more exact amount based on your body size.

Q2. How long will 1.7 oz of sunscreen last me?

A: A 1.7 ounce or 50 ml bottle of sunscreen lasts about 8 to 9 weeks if you only use it on your face. It lasts about 1 to 2 days if you use it on your whole body. The exact time depends on how many days you are in the sun, how many hours each day, and how big your body is.

Q3. How many sunscreen bottles do I need for a week vacation?

A: For a one week trip with 3 hours of sun each day, you need about 1.5 bottles of 5 fl oz or 150 ml sunscreen for your body. We suggest taking 2 bottles to be safe. The amount changes based on your height, weight, and how much sun you actually get.

Q4. What does SPF 30 mean? Is it 30 minutes?

A: No, SPF does not mean minutes. SPF shows how much longer you can stay in the sun before you burn compared to no sunscreen. For example, if your skin burns after 2 minutes without sunscreen, SPF 30 lets you stay 60 minutes. That is 30 times 2 minutes. The rule is: Protection time equals SPF times your burning time.

Q5. When should I put more sunscreen on?

A: You should put more sunscreen on every 2 hours. You should also put more on right after you swim, sweat, or dry off with a towel. Even sunscreen that says it is water resistant needs to be put on again to keep working.

Q6. What SPF number should I pick?

A: Doctors say to use at least SPF 30 every day. SPF 30 stops about 97 percent of UVB rays. SPF 50 stops about 98 percent. The difference is small. So SPF 30 is usually enough if you put it on right and put more on every 2 hours.
