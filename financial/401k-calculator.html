401(k) Information

A 401(k) is a retirement savings plan in the U.S. with tax benefits available through employers. Named after subsection 401(k) in the Internal Revenue Code, it allows employees to contribute pre-tax income to their retirement accounts. The 2025 contribution limit is $23,500 for those under 50, $31,000 for those 50+, and $34,750 for those 60-63.

Pros of 401(k)

  • Tax-deferred growth
  • Employer matching contributions
  • High contribution limits
  • Creditor protection
  • Always tax-deductible

Cons of 401(k)

  • Limited investment options
  • Higher fees than other accounts
  • Illiquid until 59½
  • Vesting periods may apply
  • Waiting periods for participation

Early Withdrawal Rules

Early withdrawals before age 59½ typically incur a 10% penalty plus income taxes. However, exceptions exist for:

  • Qualifying medical expenses
  • First-time home purchase
  • Higher education expenses
  • Disability or death
  • Age 55+ separation from employment

Required Minimum Distributions (RMD)

Starting at age 73 (72 if you reached age 72 before Dec. 31, 2022), you must take annual distributions from your 401(k). The penalty for not taking RMDs is 50% of the required amount. RMDs are calculated based on your account balance and life expectancy.

Roth 401(k) Option

Some employers offer Roth 401(k) options where contributions are made after-tax, but qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. This can be beneficial for those who expect to be in a higher tax bracket during retirement.

401k FAQs

Is there a penalty for 401k withdrawal?

Generally, withdrawals before age 59½ incur a 10% early withdrawal penalty plus income taxes, unless you qualify for an exception (for example, disability or separation from service at age 55+).

Can I withdraw 401k at 59½?

Yes. After 59½, the 10% early withdrawal penalty no longer applies. Traditional 401(k) withdrawals may still be taxed as ordinary income.
